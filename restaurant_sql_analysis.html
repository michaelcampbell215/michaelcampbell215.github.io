Key Queries & Insights

‚ùì Question 1: What are the busiest days and times for the restaurant?

This query identifies peak customer traffic patterns by counting distinct orders, first by day of the week and then by hour within each day. The use of `DAYOFWEEK()` in the `ORDER BY` clause ensures the results are presented in a natural, chronological order rather than alphabetically.


        SELECT 
        DAYNAME(order_date) AS day_of_week, 
        HOUR(order_time) AS hour_of_day, 
        COUNT(DISTINCT order_id) AS number_of_orders 
        FROM 
        fact_orders 
        GROUP BY 
        DAYOFWEEK(order_date), 
        day_of_week, 
        hour_of_day 
        ORDER BY 
        DAYOFWEEK(order_date), 
        number_of_orders DESC;

üí° Result & Insight:

The analysis revealed that lunchtimes on Mondays, Fridays, and Sundays are the absolute peak periods. This insight allows management to optimize staffing schedules and pre-prep popular dishes to handle the rush, improving service speed and customer satisfaction.

‚ùì Question 2: How can we segment the menu for strategic analysis?

This advanced query uses multiple CTEs and the `NTILE(2)` window function to segment the entire menu into a 2x2 matrix based on popularity and revenue. Each item is categorized as a "Star," "Workhorse," "Puzzle," or "Dud," providing a clear framework for strategic decision-making.


        WITH total_order_revenue AS (
        SELECT 
            m.item_name, 
            COUNT(o.order_id) AS total_orders, 
            SUM(m.price) AS total_revenue 
        FROM 
            dim_menu_items m 
            INNER JOIN fact_orders o ON m.menu_item_id = o.item_id 
        GROUP BY 
            m.item_name
        ), 
        ItemRanks AS (
        SELECT 
            item_name, 
            total_orders, 
            total_revenue, 
            NTILE(2) OVER (
            ORDER BY 
                total_orders DESC
            ) AS order_ntile, 
            NTILE(2) OVER (
            ORDER BY 
                total_revenue DESC
            ) AS revenue_ntile 
        FROM 
            total_order_revenue
        ) 
        SELECT 
        item_name, 
        total_orders, 
        total_revenue, 
        CASE WHEN order_ntile = 1 
        AND revenue_ntile = 1 THEN 'Star' WHEN order_ntile = 1 
        AND revenue_ntile = 2 THEN 'Workhorse' WHEN order_ntile = 2 
        AND revenue_ntile = 1 THEN 'Puzzle' WHEN order_ntile = 2 
        AND revenue_ntile = 2 THEN 'Dud' END AS item_category 
        FROM 
        ItemRanks 
        ORDER BY 
        item_category, 
        total_revenue DESC;

üí° Result & Insight:

The most popular item (Hamburger) is not the most profitable. The analysis identified the "Korean Beef Bowl" as a key "Star" and the entire Italian category as a high-revenue "Puzzle." This segmentation directly informs which items to promote, re-price, or potentially remove from the menu.

‚ùì Question 3: Do customers pair items from the same cuisine or mix-and-match?

This query uses a self-join to find which items are most frequently ordered together. By joining to the menu table twice, it retrieves the category for each item in the pair, allowing us to identify "Same-Category" vs. "Cross-Category" (Fusion) purchasing behavior.


WITH order_items AS (
  SELECT 
    o.order_id, 
    o.item_id, 
    m.item_name, 
    m.category 
  FROM 
    dim_menu_items m 
    INNER JOIN fact_orders o ON m.menu_item_id = o.item_id
), 
item_pairs AS (
  SELECT 
    a.item_name AS item_1, 
    a.category AS category_1, 
    b.item_name AS item_2, 
    b.category AS category_2 
  FROM 
    order_items a 
    JOIN order_items b ON a.order_id = b.order_id 
  WHERE 
    a.item_id < b.item_id
) 
SELECT 
  item_1, 
  category_1, 
  item_2, 
  category_2, 
  COUNT(*) as pair_frequency 
FROM 
  item_pairs 
GROUP BY 
  item_1, 
  category_1, 
  item_2, 
  category_2 
ORDER BY 
  pair_frequency DESC 
LIMIT 
  5;

üí° Result & Insight:

A top pairing was an American "Hamburger" with an Asian "Edamame." This surprising cross-category behavior reveals a huge marketing opportunity: creating and promoting unique "Fusion Combos" that cater to the adventurous tastes of the customer base.

1. Building a Strong Foundation

Every good analysis starts with clean data. The first step was to take the raw sales information and organize it into a clean, reliable structure. This meant connecting the menu details (like price and category) to every single order, ensuring our analysis was built on a solid and accurate foundation.

2. Answering the Big Questions

Once the data was organized, I started asking the most important business questions: When are we busiest? What are our most popular dishes? Where is our revenue really coming from? Answering these gave us a clear picture of the restaurant's day to day performance and customer habits.

3. Uncovering Hidden Patterns

To find deeper insights, I looked for hidden patterns in the sales data. I analyzed which dishes customers love to order together, revealing natural pairings and combo opportunities. I also developed a system to automatically categorize every menu item from "Stars" to "Duds" based on its unique sales and revenue performance.

4. From Data to Decisions

  • Insight 1: I discovered that the most popular items aren't always the most profitable. While the Hamburger sells the most, the Italian dishes generate the most revenue overall.
  • Insight 2: The restaurant's busiest times are highly concentrated around lunchtime on Mondays and weekends, providing a clear window for focused marketing and operational efforts.
  • Insight 3: A surprising trend emerged where customers love mixing cuisines, like pairing an American Hamburger with Asian Edamame.

5. A Clear Path to Growth

  • Menu Strategy:
    • Capitalize on high-revenue items by promoting the entire Italian category and "Star" dishes like the Korean Beef Bowl.
    • Increase the profitability of popular items like the Hamburger by bundling them in combo deals.
  • Marketing Strategy:
    • Launch a unique "Fusion Combo" campaign to capitalize on the cross cuisine trend, setting the cafe apart from competitors.
    • Create targeted lunch specials to maximize revenue during the identified peak hours.
